THE NUMBERS

Molecular properties & drug-likeness

The 28 properties the app computes, grouped into seven families — and the famous drug-likeness rules that judge whether a compound sits in typical oral-drug space.

What a "property" is here

A molecular property is a single number (or short label) that describes one trait of a molecule — its weight, its greasiness, how many places it can form a hydrogen bond, and so on. None of these requires any lab work: they are all calculated straight from the structure you type or upload. The app reports 28 of them, and to keep things tidy it sorts them into seven families (called "groups" in the app's menu).

Two quick conventions used throughout this page:

"High" / "Low" = the practical direction for a drug Å² = square ångströms, a tiny area unit g/mol = the unit of molecular weight
Where these come from

Every value is computed from the structure using the open-source cheminformatics toolkit RDKit (the same engine many pharma pipelines use), so the numbers match what you would get in KNIME, Pipeline Pilot, or a typical in-house script. The app simply gathers them in one place with plain-English explanations.

The six you'll look at most

If you only ever read a handful of numbers, these are the ones medicinal chemists reach for first. Each card says what the number means in plain words and what high or low values imply for a drug candidate.

Molecular Weight (MW)

How heavy the molecule is, in g/mol — a proxy for size. Low–moderate (≤ 500) is easier to absorb by mouth; high tends to absorb poorly and clear quickly.

LogP (greasiness)

Grease-vs-water balance. High = greasy (good at crossing membranes but risks poor solubility & off-target stickiness); low/negative = water-loving (dissolves well but may not get through a membrane).

TPSA

Topological Polar Surface Area — the total "polar" patch on the surface, in Å². High (> 140) struggles to cross the gut wall or the brain barrier; low crosses membranes easily (sometimes too easily into the brain).

H-bond Donors & Acceptors

The "handshake points" that grip a target (and water). Few = crosses membranes more easily; many = stronger water/target binding but poorer absorption.

Rotatable Bonds

How "floppy" the molecule is — its flexibility. Low (≤ 10) tends to bind cleanly and absorb well; high can wiggle into many shapes, often hurting oral uptake and binding precision.

QED

A single drug-likeness score from 0 to 1 blending several traits. Closer to 1 = more drug-like overall; near 0 = far from typical oral drugs. A quick one-glance verdict.

Why MW and LogP appear twice

Molecular Weight and LogP show up in both the Basic and Lipinski families. That's intentional — they are core descriptors and two of the four Rule-of-Five ingredients. It's the same value, just grouped where it's useful.

The Basic family in plain words

Molecular Weight (MW)

The combined weight of every atom, in grams per mole (g/mol). It's the simplest stand-in for "how big is this molecule." Low–moderate MW (roughly ≤ 500) is generally easier for the body to absorb and move around; high MW often means poor oral absorption and faster clearance. Aspirin (CC(=O)OC1=CC=CC=C1C(=O)O) weighs about 180 g/mol — comfortably small.

Exact Mass (monoisotopic mass)

The mass calculated using the single most common isotope of each element (for example carbon-12 exactly, not the average carbon). MW uses average atomic weights; Exact Mass is the precise number a high-resolution mass spectrometer expects to see. Use it to match or confirm a peak in a mass spec readout; use MW for size/Rule-of-Five thinking. There is no "good" or "bad" direction — it's an identity number.

Molecular Formula

The plain element tally, like C₉H₈O₄ for aspirin — how many carbons, hydrogens, oxygens, and so on. It's not a score; it's a quick sanity check that the structure you entered is the one you meant, and a handy label when comparing isomers (same formula, different arrangement).

LogP — grease vs water-loving

LogP measures how a molecule splits itself between oil and water. Technically it's the logarithm of the partition coefficient — the ratio of how much sits in an oily layer (octanol) versus a water layer.

LogP = log10 ( concentration in oil ÷ concentration in water )
💡
In plain words: a higher LogP means the molecule prefers grease over water. Around 0 it's evenly balanced; +3 to +5 is quite greasy; negative is strongly water-loving. Greasy compounds slip through fatty cell membranes more easily but tend to dissolve poorly and stick to off-target proteins; water-loving compounds dissolve nicely but may never cross a membrane to reach the target. The sweet spot for many oral drugs is roughly LogP 1–3.

TPSA — polar surface, the membrane gatekeeper

TPSA (Topological Polar Surface Area) adds up the surface area belonging to polar atoms — mainly oxygen and nitrogen and the hydrogens attached to them — reported in square ångströms (Å²). It is one of the best simple predictors of whether a molecule can cross biological barriers.

💡
In plain words: think of TPSA as how much of the molecule's "skin" is sticky and water-grabbing. The more sticky surface, the harder it is to slip through a greasy membrane. Rough guides chemists use: TPSA ≤ 140 Å² for decent gut absorption (oral uptake), and TPSA ≤ ~90 Å² if you want it to reach the brain (cross the blood–brain barrier). A low TPSA crosses easily — which is good for a brain drug but a liability if you're trying to keep a compound out of the brain.

Atom Count & Bond Count

Straightforward tallies of how many atoms and how many bonds make up the molecule (including hydrogens by default). They're rarely a decision-maker on their own, but they give a feel for raw size and connectivity and feed into other measures. Bigger counts simply mean a bigger, more elaborate structure.

The Lipinski family

These four are the raw ingredients of the Rule of Five. They're explained fully in the drug-likeness rules below; here is the plain meaning of the two that are unique to this family.

H-bond Donors (HBD) & H-bond Acceptors (HBA) — the handshake points

A hydrogen bond is a gentle, reversible "handshake" between molecules. A donor offers a hydrogen (think –OH or –NH groups); an acceptor receives one (think the lone pairs on oxygen or nitrogen). Counting them tells you how many grip points the molecule has — both for clinging to its target and for clinging to surrounding water.

💡
In plain words: more handshake points usually means stronger, more specific binding to a target — but also more attraction to water, which makes it harder to cross a greasy membrane. Few donors/acceptors = better membrane crossing; many = better binding/solubility but tougher absorption. The Rule of Five caps donors at 5 and acceptors at 10 for oral drugs.

The Drug-likeness family

This family contains roll-up verdicts rather than raw measurements. Lipinski violations and Veber criteria are pass/fail rule checks (detailed in the drug-likeness rules below). The two unique to this family are the scoring numbers below.

QED — one number for "drug-likeness"

QED (Quantitative Estimate of Drug-likeness) bundles several properties — weight, greasiness, polar surface, hydrogen-bonding, rings, and alerts — into a single score between 0 and 1.

QED = blended "desirability" of MW, LogP, TPSA, H-bonds, rings, … → a single 0–1 value
💡
In plain words: QED answers "how typical of a marketed oral drug is this?" in one glance. Closer to 1 = it looks like a clean, drug-like molecule; near 0 = it has features marketed drugs rarely show. It's a fast triage score, not a guarantee — treat it as a thermometer, not a verdict.

Synthetic Accessibility (SA) — how hard is it to make?

SA estimates how difficult a molecule would be to synthesize in the lab, on a scale from 1 (easy) to 10 (hard). It rewards common, simple fragments and penalizes rare ring systems, many stereocenters, and unusual chemistry.

💡
In plain words: a low SA score (near 1–3) means a chemist could likely make it quickly and cheaply — great for fast follow-up. A high SA score (7–10) warns that it may take many steps, exotic reagents, or tricky purifications. It's a planning hint for prioritizing which "great on paper" ideas are actually worth chasing.

The Rules family

Beyond Lipinski's Rule of Five, three other published filters each define their own "drug-like" window across properties like weight, greasiness, atom counts, and polar surface. In this app each returns a simple pass / fail verdict.

Ghose filter Egan filter Muegge filter

Each was derived from a different reference set of known drugs, so they don't always agree. Treat agreement across several as reassuring, and a lone failure as a prompt to look closer rather than a rejection. Full ranges and how to read disagreements live in the drug-likeness rules below.

The Rings family

Rings give a molecule its backbone and shape. The app reports three different ways of counting them, and the distinction matters.

○

Ring count

The total number of individual rings. A fused two-ring system like naphthalene counts as two rings.

⬡

Aromatic ring count

How many of those rings are aromatic — flat, electron-rich rings like benzene. Many aromatic rings can raise greasiness and stacking/off-target risk.

⬢

Ring-system count

How many separate ring assemblies there are. Naphthalene's two fused rings form one ring system; two unconnected benzene rings would be two.

Why three counts, not one

Ring count tells you total rings; ring-system count tells you how those rings are clustered. Together they describe rigidity and shape better than either alone. Too many flat aromatic rings is a known liability — it often pushes greasiness up and three-dimensional character (next section) down.

The Complexity family

Fraction sp3 carbons (Fsp3) — how three-dimensional is it?

Carbons come in two flavors here: sp3 carbons are the "puckered," tetrahedral ones found in chains and saturated rings, while flat aromatic/double-bonded carbons are sp2. Fsp3 is simply the share of carbons that are sp3.

Fsp3 = (number of sp3 carbons) ÷ (total number of carbons)
💡
In plain words: Fsp3 runs from 0 (completely flat, all aromatic) to 1 (fully three-dimensional). Higher Fsp3 = more 3-D, less flat — and flatter molecules are statistically more prone to poor solubility and off-target hits. Raising Fsp3 (adding saturated, "puckered" character) is a popular way to improve a series' developability.

Rotatable bonds — flexibility

A rotatable bond is a single bond (not in a ring, not to a terminal atom) that the molecule can freely spin around. The count is a direct measure of floppiness. Low (≤ 10) usually means cleaner binding and better oral uptake; high means the molecule can twist into many shapes, which often hurts both absorption and binding precision. This is also one of the two Veber criteria.

Stereocenters — three-dimensional handedness

A stereocenter is an atom (usually a carbon) whose four different attachments can be arranged two mirror-image ways — like a left versus right hand. The count tells you how many such "handed" points exist.

💡
In plain words: every stereocenter doubles the number of possible 3-D forms (stereoisomers), and biology often cares which hand you give it — one mirror image can be the drug and the other inactive or even harmful. More stereocenters = richer 3-D shape (often desirable for selectivity) but harder and costlier to make and purify as a single, defined form. Zero means no handedness to worry about.

The Additional family

Formal charge

The molecule's net electrical charge as drawn — the sum of any built-in positive and negative charges. 0 means neutral; +1, −1, … means it carries a charge. Charged species behave very differently: they dissolve better in water but usually cross membranes poorly, which strongly affects absorption and where the compound ends up in the body.

Molar refractivity (MR)

A measure of how much the molecule bends light, which closely tracks its size and polarizability (how easily its electron cloud is distorted). In practice it's a refined "bulk" descriptor — a richer cousin of molecular weight — and it appears as one of the boundaries in the Ghose filter. Bigger, more polarizable molecules have higher MR.

Heavy-atom count

The number of non-hydrogen atoms (carbon, nitrogen, oxygen, and so on). Chemists favor it over total atom count because hydrogens are implied by the structure, so heavy-atom count is a cleaner, hydrogen-free measure of size. It's also the denominator in several ligand-efficiency metrics — see Ligand efficiency.

Reading the extremes

No single number is a verdict

A property at an extreme is a flag for attention, not a rejection. Plenty of important drugs break these comfort zones on purpose: many antibiotics carry very high TPSA and weight; some CNS drugs sit at the low-TPSA, greasy end by design; biologics and injectables ignore oral-absorption rules entirely because they don't need to survive the gut. Read properties together, in the context of how the drug will be given and where it needs to act — not one at a time against a hard cutoff.

A practical reading order

Glance at QED for an overall feel, then check MW, LogP, and TPSA together (size, grease, polar surface — the absorption trio). Use HBD/HBA and rotatable bonds to explain why absorption might suffer, Fsp3 and aromatic rings for shape/developability, and SA to judge whether a promising idea is actually worth making.

Drug-likeness rules

Famous rules of thumb that flag molecules drifting outside the range of typical oral drugs.

What "drug-likeness" really means

A drug-likeness rule is a quick gut-check: a short list of property limits that most successful, swallowable medicines tend to stay inside. If a molecule wanders far outside those limits, the rule raises a small flag — a reminder to look closer.

None of these rules measure whether a compound is potent, safe, or selective. They only ask a narrower question: "Does this molecule look like the kinds of compounds that have historically survived the journey from the gut, through the bloodstream, and into cells?" They were learned by looking back at thousands of marketed oral drugs and noticing where the bulk of them clustered.

The app reports several of these rules side by side so you can see, at a glance, whether a compound sits comfortably in "drug-like space" or is starting to drift toward the edges. Below we explain each one in plain words, the exact cut-offs the tool uses, and — crucially — when to ignore them.

First, a few terms

MW (molecular weight) = how heavy the molecule is, in daltons (g/mol). LogP = greasiness — high LogP means the molecule prefers oil over water. H-bond donor = an –OH or –NH group that can offer a hydrogen for a hydrogen bond. H-bond acceptor = an N or O that can receive one. TPSA (topological polar surface area, in Å²) = the total area of the molecule's polar patches. Rotatable bonds = single bonds the molecule can freely twist around, a measure of floppiness. All of these are defined in full in the properties sections above.

Lipinski's Rule of Five

The most famous rule of all, published by Christopher Lipinski in 1997 after studying which oral drugs made it to market. It is called the "Rule of Five" because every limit is a multiple of five. It describes the typical envelope of an orally absorbed small-molecule drug — one you can take as a pill.

MW ≤ 500 Da LogP ≤ 5 H-bond donors ≤ 5 H-bond acceptors ≤ 10
💡
In plain words: a good oral drug is usually not too heavy (under ~500 Da), not too greasy (LogP under 5 — greasy molecules dissolve poorly and stick where they shouldn't), and doesn't carry too many hydrogen-bonding handshake points (donors and acceptors). Too many of those polar handshakes make it hard to slip through the fatty membrane of a cell, so the molecule struggles to get absorbed from the gut.

How violations are counted

Each of the four limits a molecule breaks counts as one violation. So the score runs from 0 (inside all four limits) to 4 (outside all of them). The conventional reading:

ViolationsWhat it meansApp flag
0Sits squarely in classic oral drug space.Pass
1Still considered fine — one borderline value is common and acceptable.Pass
≥ 2Drifting outside the typical envelope — worth a closer look at absorption.Flag
Why "one is okay"

Lipinski's original paper allows a single violation precisely because real drugs are not perfect rectangles. A compound that is a touch heavy but otherwise ideal is rarely doomed. It is the combination of several values being off (two or more violations) that more reliably signals an absorption problem — which is why the app turns the flag amber at ≥ 2.

A worked example

Take aspirin, CC(=O)OC1=CC=CC=C1C(=O)O. Its MW is about 180 Da (≤ 500 ✓), LogP about 1.2 (≤ 5 ✓), it has 1 H-bond donor (≤ 5 ✓) and 4 acceptors (≤ 10 ✓). Zero violations — a textbook drug-like small molecule, which is exactly why aspirin is the canonical example.

Veber's rules

Published by Daniel Veber and colleagues in 2002, these two add flexibility and polar surface to the picture — two things Lipinski's original four didn't cover. They were derived specifically from oral bioavailability data in rats.

TPSA ≤ 140 Å² Rotatable bonds ≤ 10
💡
In plain words: a molecule absorbs better when it isn't too floppy (10 or fewer freely-twisting bonds) and doesn't expose too much polar surface (140 Å² or less). A very floppy molecule wastes energy contorting itself and pays a penalty crossing membranes; a very polar surface acts like a coat of water that the fatty membrane repels. Both make a compound harder to absorb from the gut.
A handy TPSA shortcut

A tighter TPSA cut-off of ≤ 90 Å² is often used as a flag for whether a molecule can cross the blood–brain barrier into the central nervous system. So a brain-targeting drug wants low polar surface, while a drug meant to stay out of the brain may deliberately keep TPSA high. Veber's 140 Å² is the looser, general oral-absorption line.

Three more published filters

The app also reports three older filters that pre-date or parallel Lipinski. Each draws its own box from its own training set, so a compound can pass one and fail another — that disagreement is itself useful information.

Ghose filter

Sets both upper and lower bounds: MW 160–480, LogP −0.4 to 5.6, molar refractivity 40–130, and 20–70 atoms. Unlike Lipinski it also rejects molecules that are too small, on the logic that real drugs need a minimum size to bind.

Egan filter

A two-property "egg-shaped" zone using only LogP and TPSA (the two that most govern passive absorption). A compound is drug-like if it lands inside that egg — roughly LogP up to ~6 and TPSA up to ~130 Å².

Muegge filter

The strictest checklist, from Bayer. Adds bounds on rings, carbon count, heteroatoms, rotatable bonds, donors, and acceptors on top of MW and LogP. Designed to weed out compounds that are technically small but chemically odd.

Why show several at once?

If a molecule passes all the filters, you can be fairly confident it sits in well-trodden drug-like space. If it fails just one (say Muegge, the strictest), that's mild. If it fails most of them, that's a stronger signal it's an unusual compound — not necessarily bad, but worth understanding why before you invest in it.

QED — one drug-likeness number

The rules above give pass/fail verdicts. QED (Quantitative Estimate of Drug-likeness) instead blends several properties into a single smooth score between 0 and 1, where closer to 1 means "more like the average marketed oral drug."

QED = blended score of (MW, LogP, HBD, HBA, TPSA, rotatable bonds, aromatic rings, alerts)
💡
In plain words: instead of a hard yes/no on each limit, QED asks "how desirable is each property?" and gently rewards values near the sweet spot, penalizing values far from it. It then combines those eight gentle scores into one number. A QED near 0.9 looks very drug-like; near 0.2 looks unusual. Because it's smooth, a molecule that is slightly over a limit isn't punished as harshly as it would be by a hard rule — QED captures "almost ideal" better than a checklist can.

💡

What this shows: QED is a single sliding scale. Aspirin lands mid-scale (~0.55) — small and simple molecules often score modestly because QED rewards the balance of a typical mid-sized drug, not just passing the limits.

Synthetic Accessibility (SA)

A different kind of practical check. SA estimates how hard the molecule would be to make in the lab, on a scale of 1 to 10.

1–3 = easy to synthesize 4–6 = moderate 7–10 = hard / exotic
💡
In plain words: SA looks at how common the building-block fragments are (rewarding familiar, easy-to-buy pieces) and how awkward the structure is (penalizing many stereocenters, fused exotic ring systems, and large molecules). A low SA score is good — it means your chemists can probably make the compound without heroics. A high score warns that even a great-looking molecule might be expensive or impractical to actually produce.
Pair it with QED

QED and SA answer two different questions: "does it look like a drug?" and "can we actually build it?" A compound with high QED and low SA is the dream — drug-like and easy to make. High QED but high SA is a warning that the prize comes at a synthetic cost.

The most important caveat

Rules are guides, not gates

These filters are prioritization tools, not pass/fail gatekeepers. They were built almost entirely from oral small-molecule drugs, so they say little about anything outside that world. Plenty of important, approved drugs break them on purpose:

  • Many antibiotics (erythromycin, vancomycin) are large and polar — they violate the Rule of Five and still work.
  • Injectable and infused drugs skip the gut entirely, so absorption rules barely apply.
  • Natural products and macrocycles routinely exceed MW, donor, and rotatable-bond limits.
  • Targeted protein degraders and peptides live well outside "Rule-of-Five space" by design.

Use these flags to decide where to spend your attention — a violation says "understand why before you proceed," not "reject this molecule." A compound that breaks a rule for a good, deliberate reason is perfectly fine.

Common questions

My molecule has 2 violations — should I drop it?

Not automatically. Two violations is a flag to investigate, not a verdict. Check which limits it broke and whether there's a reason — a slightly heavy macrocycle or an injectable candidate may be entirely viable. Ligand efficiency and the interference flags give you more context for the decision.

Why do Lipinski and Muegge disagree on the same molecule?

Because they were trained on different data and check different properties. Muegge is the strictest, with extra bounds on rings, heteroatoms, and rotatable bonds. A compound can clear Lipinski's four limits yet trip one of Muegge's extra ones. The disagreement tells you the molecule is borderline on something Lipinski simply doesn't look at.

Is a higher QED always better than passing Lipinski?

They measure slightly different things. Lipinski is a hard checklist for oral absorption; QED is a smooth overall "looks like an average drug" score. A molecule can pass Lipinski yet have a modest QED (aspirin does), simply because QED also rewards balance across more properties. Read them together, not as substitutes.

Do these rules tell me if a compound is potent or safe?

No. They speak only to absorption and drug-likeness — whether a compound resembles successful oral drugs in size, greasiness, and polarity. Potency comes from assays; safety comes from toxicology. For binding-per-size efficiency, see Ligand efficiency; for assay false alarms, see Interference flags.
