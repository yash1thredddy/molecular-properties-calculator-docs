THE ANALYSES

3D regression analysis

See how one property depends on two others at the same time — fit a flat sheet through your data and measure how well it fits.

What it does

This workspace answers a very practical question: "When I change two things about a molecule, how does a third thing respond?" For example, as molecules in a series get heavier and greasier, does their activity go up, down, or stay flat? Regression is the formal way to read that combined trend off your data instead of eyeballing it.

You pick three numeric columns from your dataset — call them X, Y, and Z. The tool treats Z as the result you want to explain (the "output," also called the dependent variable) and X and Y as the two inputs that might drive it (the "predictors," or independent variables). It then fits a flat sheet — a plane — through the cloud of points so the sheet sits as close to all of them as possible. That best-fit sheet is the trend.

💡
In plain words: imagine your molecules as dots floating in a 3-D box. Left–right is X, front–back is Y, up–down is Z. Regression slides a stiff flat sheet of paper through the dots until it splits them as evenly as possible. Where the sheet tilts and how high it sits tells you the trend; how tightly the dots hug the sheet tells you how trustworthy that trend is.

"OLS" is the method used — it stands for Ordinary Least Squares. "Least squares" simply means the sheet is positioned to make the total of the squared up-and-down gaps between dots and sheet as small as possible. Squaring the gaps keeps positives and negatives from cancelling and punishes big misses harder than small ones. You don't need to compute any of this; the tool does it and hands you the answer.

The result is shown as an interactive, rotatable 3-D view (built with Three.js): your points as a scatter cloud with the fitted plane passing through them. You can spin, tilt, and zoom it with the mouse to see the trend from any angle, plus a panel of summary numbers (explained below) that put a value on how strong and how real the trend is.

"3D" means three columns, not 3-D shape

The "3D" here refers to the three properties you plot against each other, not the three-dimensional shape of a molecule. You're studying how three numbers relate, and a plane is the natural fit when there are exactly two inputs and one output.

How to use it

You'll need a dataset with at least three numeric columns — for instance a batch you've already enriched with properties (see Batch processing), or a file that already carries activity values like pIC50 alongside computed properties.

Load your data

Upload a CSV or XLSX file in the 3D Regression workspace. Each row is one molecule; the columns are the properties and measurements you'll choose from.

Pick X, Y, and Z

Choose the two input properties (X and Y) and the one output you want to explain (Z) from drop-down menus. A common setup: X = molecular weight, Y = LogP (greasiness), Z = activity (pIC50). Only numeric columns appear in the menus.

Fit the plane

The tool fits the best plane through your points and draws the rotatable 3-D view. Rows with a missing value in any of the three chosen columns are set aside automatically so they don't distort the fit — the tool tracks indices carefully so the points you see match the numbers reported.

Read the statistics

Below the plot you get a full results panel: how much of the variation the trend explains, whether the trend is statistically real, and exactly how much Z shifts per unit of X and per unit of Y. The rest of this page walks through every one of those numbers.

Which column should be Z?

Put the thing you care about predicting in Z. In structure–activity work that's almost always the activity (potency). X and Y are the structural or physico-chemical knobs you suspect control it. Swapping which column is Z changes the question you're asking, so choose deliberately.

How to read every statistic

When the fit finishes, the tool prints a full OLS summary — the same set of numbers a statistician would report. Below is every one, in plain words, with what it tells you about your compounds. The cards give the headline; the prose underneath fills in the detail.

R²

How much it explains

The share of the up-and-down spread in Z that the trend accounts for, from 0% to 100%. Higher = stronger, more useful trend.

F

Is there a trend at all?

One overall test of whether X and Y together explain Z better than random noise would.

p

Could it be luck?

The probability the pattern appeared by chance. Below 0.05 = probably real, not a fluke.

R² — how much of the variation the trend explains

R² (pronounced "R-squared") is the single most important headline number. It ranges from 0 to 1 (often shown as 0% to 100%) and answers: "Of all the way Z bounces around, what fraction does my plane account for?"

  • R² = 0.90 (90%) — the trend explains almost everything; the dots hug the plane tightly. A strong, dependable relationship.
  • R² = 0.30 (30%) — the plane catches only a small slice; most of Z's behavior is driven by something other than your two inputs (or by noise). Treat the trend as weak.
  • R² = 0.0 (0%) — X and Y tell you nothing about Z. The plane is essentially flat and useless.
R² = 1 − (leftover spread around the plane) / (total spread in Z)
💡
In plain words: start with how much Z scatters on its own. Now fit the plane and see how much scatter is left over. R² is the portion you managed to mop up. If the plane removes most of the scatter, R² is near 1; if it removes almost none, R² is near 0.

Adjusted R² — R² with an honesty correction

Plain R² has a quirk: it can only go up (or stay level) when you add more inputs, even if those inputs are useless. Adjusted R² fixes this by docking a small amount for each input the model uses. In this workspace you always use exactly two inputs (X and Y), so adjusted R² sits just below R². If the two are close, your inputs are pulling their weight; if adjusted R² is noticeably lower, one of your inputs may be adding little.

💡
In plain words: regular R² rewards you for piling on inputs even when they don't help. Adjusted R² is the "no free credit" version — it's the number to quote when you want to be fair and avoid fooling yourself.

F-statistic — the overall "is there a trend?" test

The F-statistic is a single number that tests the whole model at once: "Do X and Y together explain Z better than a flat, no-trend baseline?" A large F means the trend is strong relative to the leftover noise; an F near 1 means your inputs barely beat guessing the average every time. You read F together with its own p-value (next).

💡
In plain words: F asks the yes/no question "is anything going on here at all?" before you bother interpreting the individual slopes. A big F is a green light; a small F says the whole relationship may be noise.

p-value — the chance it's just luck

A p-value is a probability between 0 and 1. It estimates how often you'd see a pattern this strong purely by chance if there were really no relationship. Small p = unlikely to be a fluke = probably a real effect. The conventional cut-off is 0.05:

p-valuePlain readingVerdict
p < 0.001Less than 0.1% chance of being luckVery strong
p < 0.05Less than 5% chance of being luckProbably real
0.05 – 0.10Borderline; could go either waySuggestive
p > 0.10Quite plausibly just chanceNot convincing

You'll see a p-value for the overall model (tied to the F-statistic — is the whole plane meaningful?) and a separate p-value for each slope (is this particular input pulling its weight?). It's common to have a strong overall model where only one of the two inputs has a small p-value — meaning one input does most of the work.

A small p-value is not a big effect

"Real" and "large" are different things. A p-value tells you the trend is probably not luck; it says nothing about whether the trend is big enough to matter. For "how big," read the slopes below.

Coefficients (β0, β1, β2) — the levers

The fitted plane is described by an equation with three numbers, called coefficients (the Greek letter β, "beta," is just shorthand for them):

Z = β0 + β1 · X + β2 · Y
💡
In plain words: to predict Z for any molecule, start at a baseline (β0), add β1 for every unit of X, and add β2 for every unit of Y. The two slopes β1 and β2 are the levers: they tell you exactly how much the output moves when you turn each input knob.

β0 — intercept

The baseline value of Z when both X and Y are zero. It anchors the height of the plane. Often not chemically meaningful on its own (zero MW isn't a real molecule), but it's needed to position the trend.

β1 — slope on X

How much Z changes for each one-unit increase in X, holding Y fixed. Positive = Z rises as X rises; negative = Z falls. The further from zero, the stronger X's pull.

β2 — slope on Y

How much Z changes for each one-unit increase in Y, holding X fixed. Same reading as β1, but for your second input. Compare β1 and β2 to see which input matters more.

Worked example: suppose Z = activity (pIC50), X = molecular weight, Y = LogP, and the fit gives β1 = +0.003 and β2 = +0.45. That reads as: each extra unit of LogP raises predicted activity by about 0.45, while each extra unit of molecular weight raises it by only 0.003. The series is driven far more by greasiness than by size — a concrete, actionable SAR insight.

Standard errors & confidence intervals — the uncertainty

Every slope is an estimate from a limited sample, so it carries uncertainty. The summary reports two ways to see that uncertainty:

  • Standard error (SE) — the typical wobble in a slope. A small SE next to a slope means the slope is pinned down precisely; a large SE means it could easily be bigger or smaller than the number shown.
  • Confidence interval (CI) — usually the "95% CI," a low-to-high range that very likely contains the true slope. If the 95% CI is, say, +0.30 to +0.60, you can be fairly sure the real effect of that input is positive. If the CI straddles zero (e.g. −0.10 to +0.40), the input might have no effect at all — be cautious about claiming it does.
💡
In plain words: a slope is your best single guess; the standard error and confidence interval are the "± fuzz" around that guess. A tight range you can trust; a wide range that crosses zero means "we honestly can't tell which way this input pushes."

When to use it

3D regression shines whenever you want to put a number on a relationship rather than just look at a chart.

Structure–activity relationships (SAR)

Quantify how two structural or physico-chemical properties jointly drive potency. The slopes tell you which knob to turn — and by how much — to push activity in the right direction.

Property optimization

Understand trade-offs across a series — e.g. how solubility responds as you tune size and polarity — so you can steer a chemical series toward a better balance.

The 3-D scatter on the Visualization gallery page draws a similar plane for casual exploration; this workspace is the place to go when you need the full statistics behind it.

A low R² means a weak relationship — don't over-interpret

If R² comes back small (say under ~0.3), the plane explains little of what's going on. The slopes may still be statistically "real," but they capture only a sliver of the picture — most of Z is driven by factors not in your two inputs. Don't make design decisions on a weak fit: try different inputs, check for a non-linear pattern the flat plane can't capture, look for a confounding factor, or gather more data before trusting the trend.

What if I swap an input?

Try a few X/Y combinations and compare R² and the p-values. The pairing that gives the highest R² and small p-values on its slopes is the relationship best supported by your data. This kind of quick experimentation is exactly what the workspace is for.

Common questions

Why a plane and not a curve?

OLS regression fits the simplest shape that captures a straight-line trend in each input: a flat plane. It's easy to read and rarely overfits. If your data clearly bends (a curve rather than a slope), a flat plane will show a lower R² — that low R² is your clue that the real relationship is curved and a plane can't capture it.

What happens to rows with missing values?

Any molecule missing a value in X, Y, or Z is set aside before fitting so it can't distort the plane. The workspace carefully tracks which rows were dropped, so the points drawn in the 3-D view always line up with the molecules behind the reported statistics.

My overall model is significant but one slope isn't — what gives?

That's normal and informative: it means one input (the one with the small p-value) is doing most of the explaining, while the other contributes little. Its confidence interval will often straddle zero. You can simplify your thinking around the input that actually matters.

Does regression prove that X and Y cause Z?

No. Regression measures association, not causation. A strong fit shows the properties move together in your dataset; it doesn't guarantee that changing one will change the other in a new molecule. Use it as a strong hint to guide design and follow up with real measurements.
