THE NUMBERS

Ligand efficiency

A small molecule that binds well is doing more with less. Efficiency indices put compounds of different sizes on a level playing field.

The core idea

When you compare two compounds in a screening campaign, the simplest question is "which binds harder?" But that question quietly punishes small molecules. A big molecule has more atoms touching the target, so it almost always looks stronger on raw binding alone. That tells you very little about whether the molecule is good — only that it is large.

A ligand is simply the molecule that binds to a protein target (your candidate drug). Ligand efficiency rescales binding so that size is taken out of the comparison. Instead of asking "how much total binding power?" it asks "how much binding power per unit of molecule?"

💡
In plain words: divide how strongly a molecule binds by how big it is — power per litre, not total power. A small engine that's fiercely efficient can be worth far more than a giant engine that's merely powerful, because you still have room to make the molecule bigger and better. Ligand efficiency is the "miles per gallon" of a binding molecule.
Why "per size" and not just "stronger"?

You can almost always make a molecule bind harder by bolting on more atoms — but each extra atom also adds weight, greasiness, and metabolic liabilities. Efficiency keeps you honest: it rewards binding that comes from quality of fit, not just bulk. A molecule that wins on efficiency has the best starting point for the long road of optimization ahead.

The eight indices

The app reports eight efficiency indices, all following the AtlasCBS methodology (Abad-Zapatero & Cortés-Cabrera, 2013). They share one idea — binding strength spread across a measure of size — but each one chooses a different ruler for "size," and a few express the same idea on a different mathematical scale (more on that below). The four rulers are the count of heavy atoms, the molecular weight, the polar surface area, and the count of polar atoms. Reporting several at once lets you see a compound from more than one angle.

Four terms you'll see repeatedly, defined plainly:

Heavy atoms = every atom except hydrogen (the molecule's "real" atom count) MW = molecular weight, the molecule's mass TPSA = polar surface area, the size of the water-loving region Polar atoms = the count of nitrogen and oxygen atoms (the polar contact points)

Which one should I trust?

No single index is "the right one" — they're complementary lenses. The heavy-atom family (NBEI, nBEI, LEH) is the most common in early discovery because counting atoms is intuitive and robust. The polar family catches compounds whose binding leans on polar contacts — SEI measures it by polar surface area (TPSA), while NSEI and LEP measure it by the count of polar atoms. The mass family (BEI, mBEI) is handy when you care about the final drug's weight. If a compound scores well on most of them, that's a strong signal; if it wins on one ruler but loses on another, that disagreement is itself informative.

The shared formula

Every index follows the same shape — binding strength spread across size. "Binding strength" comes from an activity value (defined in the next section — essentially, a bigger number means a tighter binder), and "size" is whichever ruler the index uses.

efficiency = binding strength ÷ size (atoms, weight, or polar measure)
💡
In plain words: take how tightly the molecule grips its target, then spread that grip across the molecule's size. A high number means a lot of grip from a little molecule — exactly what you want early on, because there's still headroom to grow.

The most direct form uses the activity value as-is, divided by the number of non-hydrogen atoms:

NBEI = activity (pKi or pIC50) ÷ heavy-atom count
💡
In plain words: "how much binding did each atom buy me?" Add an atom that doesn't pull its weight, and this number drops — a built-in warning that you're bloating the molecule without earning more binding.
Same idea, three scales

Four indices (NSEI, NBEI, BEI, SEI) use the activity value directly, just divided by their size ruler. The other four express the same binding-per-size idea on a different scale: nBEI and mBEI put the raw binding constant on a logarithmic scale before dividing (which spreads crowded values into a fairer range), and LEH and LEP use the binding free energy (a thermodynamic restatement of the same measurement) divided by size. You don't need the math — just know that all eight reward more binding from less molecule, and a compound that looks efficient on several at once is the real deal.

What you must provide

The app already knows how to compute the size rulers from the structure. The one thing it can't invent is the binding strength — that comes from your own experiments. So for every compound you want an efficiency score for, you must supply an activity value.

An activity value: pKi or pIC50

Ki and IC50 are lab measurements of how tightly a compound binds (lower concentration to do the job = stronger binder). The "p" means negative log — so pKi = −log(Ki) and pIC50 = −log(IC50). The flip turns small concentrations into large, friendly numbers where higher = stronger. A pIC50 of 8 is a stronger binder than a pIC50 of 6.

The app fills in the size rulers

You do not need to provide molecular weight, polar surface, or atom counts. If your file already has those columns, the app uses them; if not, it computes them straight from the SMILES structure. Either way the eight indices come out automatically once an activity column is present.

Auto-detection of existing columns

To avoid recomputing values you already trust, the app auto-detects existing MW, TPSA, and heavy-atom columns in your uploaded file and reuses them. This keeps your measured or curated numbers intact and makes batch runs faster. It only falls back to computing a size ruler from the structure when the matching column is missing.

No activity, no efficiency

Efficiency is binding ÷ size. Without an activity value the numerator is missing, so the indices can't be computed for that row. A compound with structure but no measured pKi/pIC50 will still get its 28 properties — it just won't get efficiency scores until you add the activity.

Why it matters: fragments and lead optimization

Efficiency earns its keep in two everyday situations.

Fragment growth

In fragment-based discovery you start with tiny molecules (fragments) that bind weakly but efficiently. Raw binding would rank them as failures. Efficiency reveals which tiny fragments are punching above their weight — those are the ones worth growing into full leads.

Lead optimization — keep efficiency up as you add atoms

As you elaborate a hit into a lead, the molecule inevitably gets bigger. The goal isn't just "make it bind harder" — it's "make it bind harder faster than it grows." Watching an efficiency index across each analog tells you whether your added atoms are earning their keep or just adding dead weight.

Fair cross-series comparison

Two chemical series of very different sizes can't be ranked fairly on raw potency. Efficiency normalizes them, so a compact series and a bulky series can be compared on the same axis.

The golden rule of optimization

Every atom you add should pay rent. If adding a group raises potency but lowers efficiency, you've bought binding at too high a structural price — and that bill comes due later as poor absorption, faster metabolism, or weaker selectivity.

A worked comparison

Here's the punchline of the whole page in two compounds. Compound A is large and binds very tightly. Compound B is small and binds a bit less tightly. On raw potency, A wins easily. On efficiency, B wins — and B is the better starting point.

A — big, tight binder9.045Yes, stronger9.0 ÷ 45 = 0.20

In plain words: A is the powerful giant; B is the efficient compact. Each of B's 20 atoms earns 0.35 units of binding, while each of A's 45 atoms earns only 0.20. B does more with less. Because B still has room to grow (you can add atoms to push potency up), it has a higher ceiling — whereas A is already large and near its size budget. In a real campaign, B is usually the smarter molecule to optimize.

Sample plot — efficiency, side by side

Raw potency (left bars) says A is the clear winner. Efficiency (right bars) flips the story: B is doing more per atom.

What it shows: the big binder (A, blue) wins on raw potency but loses on efficiency to the smaller compound (B, red). Same data, two questions — and they give opposite answers.

Sample plot — iso-efficiency lines

This is the chemist's classic way of seeing efficiency at a glance: plot binding strength up the side and size across the bottom. Diagonal "iso-efficiency" lines connect points of equal efficiency — anything on the same line is equally efficient, and points toward the upper-left (strong binding from a small molecule) are the most efficient.

What it shows: each dot is a compound. The teal diagonal marks high efficiency, the amber diagonal lower efficiency. Compound B (red) sits on the more efficient line despite weaker binding than A (blue), because it gets there with far fewer atoms — exactly the "upper-left is best" intuition chemists chase.

How to get efficiency scores in the app

Add an activity column to your file

Include a column of pKi or pIC50 values alongside your structures (one row per compound). This is the only ingredient the app can't compute for you.

Upload in Batch Processing or a data mode

The app auto-detects the structure column and any existing MW, TPSA, or heavy-atom columns, reusing them so your curated numbers stay intact.

Select the ligand-efficiency indices

Pick the indices you want (NSEI, NBEI, BEI, SEI, nBEI, mBEI, LEH, LEP — or all eight). The app computes binding ÷ size for each compound.

Download the enriched table

Each chosen index becomes a clean numeric column you can sort, plot, or hand off to Excel, KNIME, or StarDrop. Sort by an efficiency column to surface your most promising small binders.

Reading the columns

Higher is better for every index — a larger number always means more binding per unit of size. Compare values within a series (same ruler, same target) rather than treating an absolute threshold as pass/fail; efficiency is most powerful as a relative ranking tool.
