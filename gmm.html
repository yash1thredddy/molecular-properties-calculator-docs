THE ANALYSES

Grouping your compounds (GMM)

Discover the natural sub-populations hiding in a library — automatically, without defining them yourself.

What "grouping" means — in plain words

Imagine you spread a whole compound library out on a bench. Some molecules are small and water-loving; others are big and greasy; a third batch sits somewhere in between. Even though nobody sorted them, your eye starts to see clumps — natural families of compounds that resemble one another. A group (also called a cluster) is exactly that: a sub-population of molecules that look alike on the properties you chose — for example "small & polar" versus "large & greasy."

The GMM Analysis workspace finds those clumps for you. The key word is automatically: you do not tell the tool what the groups are or how many to expect. You hand it the numbers, and it discovers the structure on its own. This is called unsupervised learning — "unsupervised" simply means nobody labelled the data in advance; the tool has no answer key, it just looks for natural togetherness.

How GMM actually decides — the "blend of bell-curves" idea

GMM stands for Gaussian Mixture Model. Let's unpack that name, because each word does real work:

  • Gaussian is the formal name for a bell-curve — the familiar hump shape where most values pile up in the middle and taper off at the edges. Heights of adults, errors in a measurement, and many molecular properties roughly follow a bell-curve.
  • Mixture means the tool assumes your data is really several bell-curves overlaid on top of each other — one bell-curve per hidden group.
  • Model means it builds a mathematical picture of where each bell-curve sits, how wide it is, and how many molecules belong to it.

So GMM imagines your library as a blend of bell-curves, then works backwards: for every molecule it asks, "Which bell-curve did you most likely come from?" and assigns it there. Because the answer is "most likely" rather than a hard yes/no, GMM can also tell you how confident it is about each assignment — which becomes one of the most useful outputs (see What you get back).

Why a chemist cares

Grouping turns a flat spreadsheet of thousands of compounds into a small number of readable families — "here are your three structural archetypes." That makes triage, sampling a diverse subset, and spotting "the odd ones out" dramatically faster.

Single property vs. multiple properties

Before the tool can group anything, you choose which numeric columns it should look at. There are two distinct modes, and the choice changes both what GMM does and what picture you get back.

Single-property mode — one column, looking for humps

What it is: you pick exactly one numeric column — say molecular weight. GMM then asks a simple question: does this single property split into more than one hump? It draws a density curve (a smoothed histogram — picture a histogram with the bars blurred into a flowing line) and reveals whether the curve has, for example, one bump of light compounds and a separate bump of heavy ones.

Why you'd use it: to understand how a single trait distributes across your library. Is your set really "one population of medium-weight molecules," or is it secretly "a fragment-sized cluster plus a lead-sized cluster glued together"? Single mode answers that at a glance.

What you see: a density histogram in real units, with each discovered hump shaded a different color and a vertical marker at each hump's center (its average value).

Multiple-property mode — several columns at once

What it is: you pick two or more numeric columns — say molecular weight and greasiness (LogP) and polar surface area (TPSA) — and GMM finds groups using all of them together, not one at a time.

Why this matters (the whole point): real chemical series almost never differ on just one axis. A compound isn't simply "heavy" — it's "heavy, greasy, and low-polarity," all at once. Those traits travel together in patterns that a single column can't see. Multi-property grouping captures the combination: it can separate "heavy-and-greasy" from "heavy-but-polar," which single-property mode (looking only at weight) would merge into one blurry lump.

How it works for you: think of each chosen property as one axis of a space. With three properties, every molecule becomes a point in a 3-trait space; molecules that sit close together in that space get grouped. (You don't have to picture the geometry — the tool measures the distances for you.) Because you can't draw more than two or three axes on a flat screen, you also pick two of your properties to serve as the X and Y axes of the scatter plot — purely for viewing. The grouping itself still used all the properties; the two you chose just decide the camera angle.

More properties = richer grouping, with a catch

Adding properties lets GMM see more nuance — but it also needs enough molecules to support that nuance, and it strongly benefits from turning on Standardize (below) so a big-number property like MW doesn't drown out a small-number one like LogP.

Every control, explained

Here is each switch, slider, and button in the GMM workspace — what it does, why it exists, and what changes on your result when you touch it.

Number-of-groups slider (2–6) and the "Auto (BIC)" button

What it does: the slider forces GMM to find exactly the number of groups you set, anywhere from 2 to 6. The Auto (BIC) button instead lets the tool pick the best number for you automatically (it tries each candidate count and chooses the winner — see How many groups? below).

Why it exists: sometimes you genuinely want a fixed number ("split my set into 3 buckets for three project teams"); other times you have no idea and want the data to tell you. The button covers the second case; the slider covers the first.

What changes if you move it:

  • Fewer groups (slide toward 2) → broader, coarser buckets. Each group is large and general; subtle sub-families get merged.
  • More groups (slide toward 6) → finer splits that pick up subtle structure — but at some point you start inventing groups that aren't really there, slicing one natural family into two just because the algorithm was told to find more.
Recommended workflow

Start with Auto (BIC) to get an honest first answer, then nudge the slider one step up and one step down to compare. If the groups stay sensible, trust them; if an extra group looks like a coin-flip split of one family, you've gone too fine.

Random seed — the fixed starting guess

What a "seed" is (plainly): GMM doesn't find its bell-curves in one shot. It makes a random first guess at where the groups might be, then refines that guess step by step until it settles. The seed is just a number that locks in that very first random guess. Same seed → same starting guess → same path → same final answer.

Why it exists — reproducibility: because the starting guess is random, two runs could land on slightly different groupings, especially for molecules sitting on a boundary. Fixing the seed makes the result reproducible: you run it today, a colleague runs it next month on the same file, and you both get the identical grouping. That is essential when results go into a report or a decision.

"Refit / new seed": this re-runs GMM from a different random starting guess. Usually you'll get the same groups; sometimes a few borderline molecules switch sides. That is not a bug — it's a robustness test:

  • Click Refit a handful of times. If the groups barely move, your grouping is robust and trustworthy — the structure is real enough to survive a different starting point.
  • If the groups jump around a lot between refits, the structure is weak. Treat the grouping with caution and consider fewer groups or more data.

"Reset seed": returns the seed to its default value so you (and everyone else) are back on the standard, reproducible run.

A seed is not a setting that "improves" the answer

Changing the seed doesn't make the grouping better or worse — it just shakes the starting point. Use refits to measure trust, not to hunt for a grouping you like better.

Standardize columns (toggle)

What it does: rescales every chosen property onto a common scale before grouping (technically, it converts each value to a z-score — how many standard steps above or below that column's average a value sits — but you don't need the math: just "put everything on the same footing").

Why it exists: properties live on wildly different scales. Molecular weight runs into the hundreds; LogP hovers around 0–5. Distance-based grouping treats "a difference of 50 in MW" and "a difference of 50 in LogP" as equally big — so without rescaling, the big-number property (MW) completely dominates and the others are practically ignored.

What changes:

  • On → every property gets a fair say in the grouping. Strongly recommended for multi-property mode.
  • Off → large-range properties dominate. Fine for single-property mode, where there's only one column anyway and you want the chart drawn in real units.

Log-transform columns (toggle)

What it does: compresses a skewed column — one with a long tail where a handful of very large values sit far out past the rest. A log transform pulls those outliers closer in so the column looks more even.

Why it exists: a few huge values can distort the bell-curve fit, dragging a group's "center" toward the extremes and producing lopsided, unintuitive groups. Evening out the tail gives more balanced, sensible clusters.

What changes: turn it on for a clearly skewed column (a long right tail) → more balanced groups. Leave it off for already-even properties. Negative-valued columns are skipped automatically (you can't take the log of a negative number), so it's safe to leave on without breaking anything.

Optional: "calculate molecular properties first"

What it does: if your uploaded file has a SMILES column (the text recipe for a structure) but few or no numeric columns to group on, this option computes properties — molecular weight, LogP, TPSA, QED, and more — straight from the structures, then clusters on those freshly made numbers.

When you'd want it: you have a plain list of structures and nothing else, and you want to group them by their physicochemical character without first running them through Batch Processing. Turn it on, let the tool generate the property columns, then select which of them to group on.

How many groups? BIC and AIC, from scratch

This is the question that makes or breaks a grouping, so we'll build the answer from nothing — assuming you've never heard of BIC or AIC.

Why you can't just pick "the best fit"

The obvious idea — "try 2, 3, 4, 5, 6 groups and keep whichever fits the data best" — doesn't work. Here's the trap: adding more groups ALWAYS fits better. Give GMM enough groups and eventually every molecule becomes its own tiny group, a perfect fit that explains nothing and tells you nothing. That failure has a name: overfitting — building a model so eager to match the data that it memorizes noise instead of capturing real structure.

The fix: a penalized scorecard

To escape the trap, we use a scorecard that rewards good fit but subtracts a penalty for every extra group. Now there's a tug-of-war: more groups improve the fit (score wants to drop) but each one costs a penalty (score wants to rise). The sweet spot is where the two balance. Lower score = better. So you compute the scorecard for 2, 3, 4, 5, 6 groups and pick the number with the lowest score.

BIC (Bayesian Information Criterion) and AIC (Akaike Information Criterion) are two such scorecards. They follow the same recipe — fit minus a penalty — but penalize differently.

BIC = (how badly it fits) + (penalty per setting the model learned) × log(number of molecules)
💡
In plain words: BIC starts with "how poorly the model explains the data," then adds a fine for complexity. Crucially, that fine grows with how much data you have (the log of the number of molecules) — so on a big library, BIC charges a steep price for each extra group and leans toward fewer, well-supported groups.
AIC = (how badly it fits) + 2 × (number of settings the model had to learn)
💡
In plain words: AIC also adds a complexity fine, but a gentler, fixed one (just 2 per setting the model learned, regardless of data size). Because the fine is milder, AIC is more willing to accept extra groups — it tends to suggest more groups than BIC.

Reading the "dip" chart

Plot the scorecard against the number of groups and you get a curve that dips down to a low point, then rises again. The bottom of the dip is your answer — the number of groups that best balances fit against simplicity.

There is no "BIC divided by AIC"

BIC and AIC are two separate scorecards, not parts of a ratio. You never divide one by the other. The phrase "BIC over AIC" means using BIC as the decider (preferring it), not a fraction. This app chooses with BIC — parsimony matters in drug discovery, where inventing groups out of noise leads to wasted chemistry — and it shows AIC alongside so you can see when they disagree. A big gap between the two minimums is a warning sign: the extra structure AIC wants is weak and uncertain, so trust BIC's more conservative call.

BIC and AIC agree

Both dip to the same number of groups. Strong evidence — your grouping is on solid ground.

They disagree

AIC wants more groups than BIC. The extra splits are shaky — go with BIC's fewer, better-supported groups.

What you get back

Once GMM has run, the workspace gives you several outputs designed to be read by a chemist, not a statistician.

Per-group summary table — in real units

For every discovered group, a row shows its average values in the original, readable units — so even if you standardized for the math, the table reports "Group 1: average MW 280, average LogP 1.9," not abstract z-scores. This is what turns "Group 1" into a sentence you can act on: "the small, polar fragment cluster."

Per-molecule group + confidence score

Every molecule gets stamped with its assigned group and a confidence score — how sure the tool is that the molecule truly belongs there, expressed as a percentage. Because GMM thinks in "most likely" terms, a molecule sitting comfortably in the heart of one group scores high (say 98%), while one stranded between two groups scores low (say 55%) — the tool is essentially flipping a coin about which family it joins.

Outlier flags

Some molecules fit no group well — they sit out in empty space, far from every bell-curve. These get an outlier flag. Outliers are often the most interesting compounds in the set (a genuinely novel scaffold) or the most suspect (a typo, a salt form, a mis-drawn structure). Either way, they deserve a human glance.

Confidence score

High % = squarely inside its group. Low % = borderline, sitting between groups — review before trusting the label.

Outlier flag

Fits no group well. Could be a breakthrough scaffold or a data error — always worth a manual look.

Plain-language interpretation and a labelled CSV download

The app writes a short plain-English summary of what it found, and lets you download an enriched CSV: your original file plus two new columns —

GMM_Group — which group each molecule landed in GMM_Confidence_% — how sure the tool is

Those columns drop straight into Excel, KNIME, or your own analysis — sort by confidence to find the borderline cases, or filter by group to pull one family.

A short worked example

Suppose you upload a 600-compound library and want to understand its make-up.

1 · Pick mode and properties

You choose multiple-property mode and select three columns: molecular weight, LogP (greasiness), and TPSA (polar surface). You turn Standardize ON so MW's big numbers don't drown out LogP.

2 · Let the tool choose the count

You click Auto (BIC). The dip chart bottoms out at 3 groups for BIC; AIC also dips near 3. They agree — good sign.

3 · Read the summary table

Group 1 averages MW 270 / LogP 1.2 / TPSA 80 — small & polar fragments. Group 2: MW 410 / LogP 3.8 / TPSA 55 — lead-like & moderately greasy. Group 3: MW 520 / LogP 5.6 / TPSA 40 — large & greasy, likely to struggle with the Rule of Five.

4 · Test robustness

You click Refit three times. The three centers barely move and only two borderline molecules switch sides — the grouping is robust.

5 · Act on the outputs

You sort the CSV by GMM_Confidence_%. The lowest-confidence rows sit between Groups 2 and 3; three compounds are flagged as outliers. You eyeball them — one is a mis-drawn structure (fix it), two are genuinely novel scaffolds worth following up.

The takeaway

In five steps you turned a flat 600-row spreadsheet into three named chemical families, a ranked list of borderline compounds, and a shortlist of outliers to investigate — none of which you had to define in advance.

Common questions

Does GMM tell me which group is "best"?

No. GMM only finds structure — it has no idea what your goal is. The summary table tells you what each group is (small & polar, large & greasy); deciding which family matters for your project is your call.

Why did a molecule's group change when I refit?

That molecule sits near a boundary between two groups, so a different random starting guess (new seed) tips it the other way. Check its confidence score — if it's low, the "switch" just reflects genuine ambiguity, not a problem.

Should I always turn Standardize on?

For multiple-property grouping, yes — otherwise the largest-range property steamrolls the rest. For single-property grouping you can leave it off, since there's only one column and you'll likely want the chart drawn in real units.

What if BIC says 3 but AIC says 5?

Go with BIC's 3. AIC's gentler penalty makes it eager to add groups; a gap that large means the extra two groups are weakly supported. The app deliberately decides with BIC for exactly this reason.

How many molecules do I need?

The more properties you group on and the more groups you ask for, the more molecules you need to support them. With only a few dozen compounds across several properties, refits will jump around — a clear sign there isn't enough data for fine grouping.
