Frequently asked questions

The questions in this section come up on day one — whether you need any technical background, and what to do when a familiar rule fires for one of your compounds.

Do I need to know how to code?

No. The calculator is a point-and-click web app. You type or paste a structure, or drag in a spreadsheet, and read the results on screen. There is no command line, no script to write, and nothing to install — it runs in your browser.

The only "language" you ever touch is the way you describe a molecule: a SMILES string (a short text recipe for a structure, like CC(=O)OC1=CC=CC=C1C(=O)O for aspirin), an InChI, or an InChI Key. Your drawing software or database almost always exports these for you. See Input formats for a gentle walk-through.

My compound breaks the Rule of Five — is it useless?

No. The Rule of Five (Lipinski) is a guide, not a gate. It flags compounds that may be harder to develop as orally absorbed drugs — molecular weight ≤ 500, LogP ≤ 5 (greasiness), hydrogen-bond donors ≤ 5, acceptors ≤ 10. Each limit you cross counts as one "violation." Zero or one violation is normal; two or more is a heads-up to look closer.

Plenty of real, approved drugs break it on purpose — many antibiotics, peptides, and anything given by injection rather than swallowed. The rule was built for one delivery route (oral) and one class of small molecules. Treat a violation as "ask why," not "discard." Full context on Drug-likeness rules.

Rule of thumb

If your project is an oral drug, watch violations closely. If it is an injectable, topical, or beyond-Rule-of-Five modality, the rule simply does not apply the way it does to classic small molecules.

A compound got a PAINS / interference flag — should I drop it?

Not automatically. An interference flag is a heads-up for human review, not a verdict. It means your molecule contains a substructure that has a history of misbehaving in assays — for example a PAINS shape (a "frequent hitter" that scores as active across many unrelated targets), a likely aggregator (a greasy compound that clumps and sequesters protein), or a reactive/redox/fluorescent group that can fool the readout.

The flag tells you which pattern matched and how severe it is, so you can decide. Good follow-ups: run an orthogonal assay (a second test with different chemistry), check a proper dose–response curve, and add a detergent control for suspected aggregators. A genuine hit will survive these checks; a false alarm usually will not. See Interference flags for each category and its recommended control.

Don't over-trust the flag either

The patterns are imperfect. A clean compound can match a PAINS shape harmlessly, and a true troublemaker can slip past. Use the flag to prioritize scrutiny, not to make the decision for you.

Grouping & GMM

These cover the GMM Analysis workspace, which discovers the natural groups (sub-populations of similar molecules) hiding in your data. The terms here — clusters, seeds, BIC, confidence — are defined in plain words as they come up.

How does the tool decide how many groups (clusters) to make?

A group (or cluster) is just a sub-population of molecules that resemble each other on the properties you chose — say "small & polar" versus "large & greasy." You have two ways to set the number of groups:

  • The slider (2–6) lets you force a specific number. Fewer groups = broad, coarse buckets; more groups = finer splits (but with a risk of inventing groups that aren't really there).
  • The "Auto (BIC)" button picks the number for you. It tries each candidate count and uses a scorecard called BIC (explained below) to choose the best-supported one.

You can't just pick "whatever fits best," because adding more groups always fits the data better — give it enough and every molecule becomes its own group, which is overfitting, not insight. So BIC rewards good fit but subtracts a penalty for every extra group. On the BIC curve, the score dips to a low point and then rises again; the bottom of the dip is the chosen number.

Sample plot: BIC drops as groups are added, bottoms out at the right number, then rises — the marked dip is the tool's pick.

What is the difference between BIC and AIC?

Both are scorecards for choosing how many groups to use. Each balances two things: how well the model fits the data, and a penalty for complexity (more groups). For both, lower is better, and you pick the number of groups with the lowest score.

BIC = (how badly it fits) + (penalty per group) × log(number of molecules)
AIC = (how badly it fits) + 2 × (number of settings the model had to learn)
💡
In plain words: both start with "how wrong is the model" and add a fine for being complicated. BIC charges a bigger fine the more data you have, so it prefers fewer, well-supported groups. AIC charges a gentler, fixed fine, so it tends to suggest more groups. They are two opinions, not a ratio you divide.

This app chooses using BIC — in drug discovery you'd rather not invent groups out of random noise — and shows AIC alongside so you can see when they disagree. A big gap between the two means the extra structure is weak or uncertain, so treat finer groupings cautiously. More on the GMM page.

What does a "confidence" of 60% mean for a molecule?

GMM doesn't just shove each molecule into a box — it estimates how sure it is. The confidence score (reported as GMM_Confidence_%) is the probability that a molecule belongs to its assigned group rather than another one.

  • High confidence (≈90–100%) — the molecule sits squarely inside one group. Clear membership.
  • Middling confidence (≈60%) — the molecule sits near a border between two groups. It was assigned to the most likely one, but it's genuinely ambiguous; a small change could flip it.
  • Low confidence — it fits no group well. These often get an outlier flag and are frequently the most interesting (a novel chemotype) or most suspect (a data error) molecules in the set.

Sample plot: a molecule landing in the overlap of two groups gets a middling confidence — it's a borderline case.

What is the random seed and why would I change it?

GMM fits its bell-curves with a search that starts from a random guess and then refines it. The seed is simply a number that fixes that starting guess.

Why it exists: because of the randomness, two runs could land on slightly different groupings. Fixing the seed makes results reproducible — you and a colleague get the exact same answer every time, which matters when you report findings.

What changes if you change it (the "Refit / new seed" button): a different starting guess. Usually you get the same groups, but borderline molecules near a boundary can shift. "Reset seed" returns to the default reproducible value.

Practical use

Click "Refit" a few times. If the groups barely move, your grouping is robust and trustworthy. If they jump around, the structure is weak — be cautious about reading too much into the groups.

My GMM groups changed slightly between runs — is that a problem?

Small shifts are normal and tell you something useful. Because the fit starts from a random guess (see the seed question above), a different starting point can move a few borderline molecules across a group boundary. This is exactly the robustness check we recommend.

  • Only borderline (low-confidence) molecules move → your grouping is solid. The core of each group is stable; only the genuinely ambiguous cases waver.
  • Groups reshuffle wholesale, or the number of groups changes run to run → the data doesn't have strong natural structure on those properties. Don't over-interpret the groups; consider different properties, turning on Standardize, or collecting more data.

Fix the seed when you want one stable, reportable answer; vary it deliberately when you want to test how trustworthy the grouping is.

What is the difference between single-property and multiple-property grouping?

It's a choice of how many traits the grouping looks at.

  • Single-property mode — you pick one column, say molecular weight. The tool draws a density curve and reveals whether that single trait splits into humps (e.g. a cluster of light compounds and a cluster of heavy ones). Use it to understand how one property distributes.
  • Multiple-property mode — you pick two or more columns (say weight and greasiness and polar surface) and the tool groups molecules using all of them at once. Real chemical series differ along several axes simultaneously: a compound isn't just "heavy," it's "heavy, greasy, and low-polarity." Multi-property grouping captures those combined patterns that one property can't see. The scatter output lets you choose which two properties form the X/Y axes just for viewing, colored by group.
More properties, richer picture

Multi-property grouping is more powerful but needs enough data and benefits a lot from turning on Standardize so no single big-number property dominates. See the next question.

What does "standardize columns" do?

Standardize rescales every property to a common scale before grouping. It exists to solve a fairness problem: properties have wildly different ranges. Molecular weight is in the hundreds; LogP is around 0–5. Without rescaling, the big-number property completely dominates the grouping and the others are basically ignored.

💡
In plain words: it converts each value into "how many steps above or below average" (a z-score) so a 200-unit difference in weight and a 2-unit difference in LogP count comparably. Every property gets a fair say.
  • ON — recommended for multiple-property grouping, where you want each property to matter equally.
  • OFF — fine for single-property work, where you want the chart in real units (real molecular weights, not rescaled numbers).
When should I log-transform?

A log-transform compresses a skewed column — one where a handful of very large values are stretched far away from the rest. Squashing that long tail closer to the pack helps the bell-curve fit behave and produces more balanced, sensible groups.

Sample plot: a lopsided distribution (left) becomes more even after a log-transform (right).

  • Turn it ON for a column with a long right tail (a few enormous values dwarfing the rest).
  • Leave it OFF for properties that are already fairly even.

Columns with negative values are skipped automatically, since you can't take the log of a negative number.

Inputs, files & compatibility

What you can feed in, what comes back out, and whether it all plays nicely with the rest of your toolkit.

Can I trust the InChI Key lookup if I'm offline?

An InChI Key is a short, fixed-length fingerprint of a structure (for example BSYNRYMUTXBXSQ-UHHWGUDFSA-N for aspirin). Unlike SMILES or InChI, the key cannot be expanded back into a structure by itself — it's a one-way hash. To turn a key into a usable molecule, the tool must look it up in an online database: it queries the NIH CIR resolver first, then falls back to PubChem.

So no — if you're offline, InChI Key input won't work, because there's nothing local to expand it against. SMILES and InChI, by contrast, are self-contained and work without any internet connection.

Working offline?

Convert your keys to SMILES or InChI while you have a connection, then work from those. Online lookups are also rate-limited and cached, so repeated keys resolve quickly without hammering the services. See Input formats.

What file types can I upload and how big?

The app accepts CSV and XLSX (Excel) spreadsheets. It automatically detects the column holding your structures by looking for common names — SMILES, smiles, SMI, canonical_smiles, and similar.

  • One file at a time per workspace — uploading a new file replaces the previous one in that mode.
  • Batch processing is built for large sets — thousands of molecules — using parallel processing and a progress bar.
  • If a row has a bad structure, it's reported individually and doesn't crash the whole batch; the good rows still process.
FormatExtensionBest for
Comma-separated values.csvUniversal exports from any tool
Excel workbook.xlsxNative spreadsheets with formatting
Will my results work in Excel / KNIME / StarDrop?

Yes. Downloads are plain, clean spreadsheets — your original columns plus new ones with the calculated values. The output is ordinary numeric columns, not a special file format, so it opens and behaves correctly in Excel, KNIME, Pipeline Pilot, and StarDrop, as well as Python/pandas, R, or anything else that reads CSV/XLSX.

The GMM workspace, for example, adds tidy columns like GMM_Group and GMM_Confidence_% that you can sort, filter, and pivot like any other field. Nothing is locked into the app.

Regression & the scores

Reading the statistics from 3D regression and understanding the single drug-likeness number, QED.

What does a low R² in the regression mean?

R² (R-squared) measures how much of the up-and-down variation in your target property the fitted plane actually explains. It runs from 0% to 100%.

💡
In plain words: if R² = 80%, the two properties you chose explain 80% of why the target moves, and 20% is left to other factors or noise. A low R² (say 10–20%) means your chosen properties barely explain the target — the cloud of points doesn't lie near the plane.

Sample plot: tight points hug the line (high R²); a loose cloud sits far from it (low R²).

A low R² isn't a bug — it's an honest message that those properties don't drive the outcome much. Try different inputs, and check the p-values too: a p-value below 0.05 means the pattern is probably real (not luck), even if the overall fit is modest.

What is QED?

QED stands for Quantitative Estimate of Drug-likeness. It rolls several physical traits — weight, greasiness, polar surface, hydrogen-bond counts, rotatable bonds, aromatic rings, and interference alerts — into a single score between 0 and 1. Closer to 1 = more drug-like (in line with marketed oral drugs); closer to 0 = less so.

It's handy as a quick, one-number summary to rank or filter a list when you don't want to juggle eight separate properties. But like every other score here it's a guide: a low QED doesn't doom a compound, and a high QED doesn't guarantee success. See Molecular properties for the full breakdown.

~0.0–0.3 less drug-like ~0.3–0.6 borderline ~0.6–1.0 drug-like
What does a p-value below 0.05 actually mean?

A p-value answers one question: "if there were really no relationship, how likely is it I'd see a pattern this strong just by chance?" A small p-value means "very unlikely to be luck — probably a real effect."

💡
In plain words: p < 0.05 is the common cutoff for "probably real." It means there's less than a 5% chance the pattern is a fluke. A large p-value (say 0.4) means the apparent relationship could easily be random — don't lean on it.

In the regression output, each slope (coefficient) gets its own p-value, so you can see which property is pulling its weight and which is just along for the ride. Pair it with R² (overall fit) and the confidence intervals (the plausible range for each slope) for the full picture on the regression page.

A note on every number here

Properties, rules, flags, scores, and groupings are decision aids, not decisions. They help you prioritize and ask better questions — the chemistry, the assay, and your judgment make the call.

A – C

TermIn plain words
Adjusted R-squaredA fairer version of R-squared (see below) that does not reward a model just for using more inputs. If adding a property does not genuinely help, adjusted R-squared goes down — so it is the more honest "how well does this fit" score. See 3D regression.
AggregatorAn interference flag for greasy compounds that clump into tiny particles in water and trap protein, producing a fake "inhibition" that disappears when detergent is added. A common cause of false hits in screening.
AIC (Akaike Information Criterion)A scorecard for choosing how many groups a model should have. It rewards good fit but subtracts a gentle, fixed penalty for every extra setting the model uses; lower is better. Tends to suggest slightly more groups than BIC. See Grouping (GMM).
Aromatic ringA flat, especially stable ring of atoms with shared electrons (a benzene ring is the classic example). Counting them gauges how "flat" and rigid a molecule is. See Molecular properties.
BIC (Bayesian Information Criterion)The scorecard this app uses to pick how many groups fit the data best. Like AIC it rewards good fit and penalizes extra groups, but its penalty grows with how much data you have, so it prefers fewer, well-supported groups — guarding against inventing groups out of noise. Lower is better. See Grouping (GMM).
Bell-curve (Gaussian)The familiar symmetric "hump" shape that describes how many measurements naturally spread around an average. GMM models your data as a blend of several of these humps, one per group. "Gaussian" is just the formal name for a bell-curve.
Box plotA chart that summarizes a set of numbers as a box (the middle half of the values) with a line for the middle value and whiskers reaching to the typical range, so you can compare spreads at a glance. See Visualization gallery.
ClusterA sub-population of molecules that resemble each other on the chosen properties — for example "small & polar" versus "large & greasy." GMM finds clusters automatically; you do not define them in advance.
Coefficient / slopeIn a regression, the "lever" attached to each input: how much the result changes when that one property goes up by one unit, holding the others fixed. A bigger coefficient means that property pushes the outcome harder. See 3D regression.
Confidence intervalA range that a regression estimate is likely to fall within. A narrow range means the tool is fairly sure of the number; a wide range means there is more uncertainty. See 3D regression.
Confidence score (GMM)For each molecule, how sure the tool is that it belongs to its assigned group, shown as a percentage. A high score means it sits firmly inside one group; a low score means it sits between groups and could go either way. See Grouping (GMM).
CovarianceA measure of whether two properties tend to move together (both rising, or one rising as the other falls) or change independently. GMM uses it to describe the shape and tilt of each group's bell-curve.

D – I

TermIn plain words
DensityHow tightly molecules are packed along a property's scale. A density curve is a smooth line that rises where many compounds share similar values and dips where few do — its humps reveal hidden sub-groups. See Grouping (GMM).
Egan filterA published drug-likeness rule that flags compounds outside a sensible window of greasiness (LogP) and polar surface area (TPSA), aimed at predicting good absorption.
Exact MassThe precise mass of a molecule calculated using the exact mass of each atom's most common isotope. It is the number a high-resolution mass spectrometer expects to see, slightly different from the everyday average molecular weight. See Molecular properties.
F-statisticA single number that tests whether your regression as a whole explains anything real, rather than just noise. A large F (paired with a small p-value) means the overall pattern is probably genuine. See 3D regression.
FluorophoreA part of a molecule that glows when light shines on it. Flagged as an interference risk because such compounds can drown out a fluorescence-based assay's signal and look like a hit when they are not.
Formal chargeThe net electrical charge on a molecule once you tally the pluses and minuses on its atoms. It affects solubility and how the molecule interacts with its target. See Molecular properties.
Fraction sp3 (Fsp3)The share of carbon atoms that are "three-dimensional" (saturated, with four single bonds) rather than flat. A higher Fsp3 means a more 3-D, less flat molecule, which often correlates with better drug-like behavior. See Molecular properties.
Ghose filterA published drug-likeness rule defining comfortable ranges for size, greasiness, refractivity, and atom count that typical small-molecule drugs fall within.
GMM (Gaussian Mixture Model)The method behind the grouping workspace. It assumes your data is a blend ("mixture") of several bell-curves and works out where each one sits, then assigns every molecule to the bell-curve it most likely came from — discovering natural groups for you. See Grouping (GMM).
H-bond acceptor (HBA)A spot on a molecule (usually an oxygen or nitrogen) that can receive a hydrogen bond — a gentle "handshake" with water or a protein. More acceptors generally means more water-loving. See Molecular properties.
H-bond donor (HBD)A spot on a molecule with a hydrogen it can offer for a hydrogen bond (think of N–H or O–H groups). Donors and acceptors together gauge how a molecule grips water and its target. See Molecular properties.
HeatmapA colored grid showing how strongly every property relates to every other one — warm colors for strong relationships, cool for weak — so patterns across many columns jump out at once. See Visualization gallery.
Heavy atomAny atom that is not hydrogen (carbon, nitrogen, oxygen, and so on). The heavy-atom count is a simple, hydrogen-free measure of molecular size, used in several efficiency metrics. See Molecular properties.
HistogramA bar chart that shows how often values fall into each range, revealing the shape of a single property's distribution (where most compounds sit, and whether there are gaps or extremes). See Visualization gallery.
InChIA standard text "barcode" for a molecule. Unlike a casual SMILES string, the same molecule always produces the same InChI, making it ideal for unambiguous identification. See Input formats.
InChI KeyA short, fixed-length fingerprint derived from the InChI. It is handy for database lookups but does not contain the full structure, so the app looks it up online (via the NIH resolver, with PubChem as backup) to recover the molecule. See Input formats.

L – Q

TermIn plain words
Ligand efficiencyA family of scores that divide how strongly a compound binds by its size, so a small, lean binder is not unfairly beaten by a big one that only binds tightly because it is large — "binding power per unit of molecule." See Ligand efficiency.
Lipinski Rule of FiveA quick gut-check for oral drug-likeness: molecular weight ≤ 500, LogP ≤ 5, hydrogen-bond donors ≤ 5, acceptors ≤ 10. Each broken limit counts as one violation; two or more is a flag. A guide, not a hard gate. See Drug-likeness rules.
Log-transformAn optional step that compresses a lopsided column so a few very large values are pulled closer to the rest. It makes skewed data more even, which helps GMM fit sensible bell-curves. See Grouping (GMM).
LogPA measure of greasiness versus water-loving: high LogP means the molecule prefers fat/oil, low (or negative) means it prefers water. It strongly influences absorption and membrane crossing. See Molecular properties.
Michael acceptorA reactive chemical group that readily bonds to sulfur-containing parts of proteins. Because it can grab proteins non-specifically, it is flagged as a thiol-reactive interference risk.
Molar refractivityA measure related to a molecule's size and how strongly it bends light, used as a proxy for overall bulk and polarizability in some drug-likeness filters. See Molecular properties.
Molecular Weight (MW)The average total mass of a molecule, adding up the average mass of every atom. A basic size measure; bigger molecules are often harder to absorb. See Molecular properties.
Muegge filterA published drug-likeness rule with its own ranges for size, polarity, ring count, and flexibility, designed to separate drug-like from non-drug-like molecules.
OutlierA data point that does not fit the overall pattern — in GMM, a molecule that belongs to no group well. Outliers are worth a closer look: they are often either the most interesting or the most suspect compounds. See Grouping (GMM).
p-valueThe chance that a pattern you see is just luck rather than real. A small p-value (below 0.05 is the common cutoff) means the result is probably genuine; a large one means it could easily be coincidence. See 3D regression.
PAINSPan-Assay Interference Compounds — about 480 published chemical shapes that tend to score as hits across many unrelated assays ("frequent hitters"). A PAINS flag is a heads-up to check a hit carefully, not a verdict. See Interference flags.
pKi / pIC50Activity values that express how strongly a compound acts, on a convenient scale where bigger numbers mean more potent (each whole step is roughly tenfold stronger). Ligand-efficiency scores need one of these as the "binding strength" input. See Ligand efficiency.
QED (Quantitative Estimate of Drug-likeness)A single score from 0 to 1 that blends several drug-like traits into one number; closer to 1 means more drug-like overall. A convenient quick read. See Molecular properties.

R – Z

TermIn plain words
R-squared (R²)How much of the up-and-down variation in your data a model explains, from 0% (explains nothing) to 100% (explains it all). Higher usually means a better fit, but it should always be read alongside adjusted R-squared and the p-value. See 3D regression.
Random seedA number that fixes the random starting point GMM uses when it searches for groups. Setting it makes results reproducible — you and a colleague get the exact same grouping every time. Changing it ("Refit / new seed") tests robustness: if the groups barely move, they are trustworthy. See Grouping (GMM).
Redox-activeAn interference flag for compounds (such as quinones, catechols, and nitro groups) that shuttle electrons and generate reactive species, which can mess with assay chemistry and create false signals.
RegressionA method that fits a simple relationship — here, a flat plane — through your data so you can describe how one outcome depends on other properties, and estimate how strong each influence is. See 3D regression.
Rotatable bondA single bond that the molecule can freely twist around. Counting them measures flexibility; too many floppy bonds can hurt how well a drug is absorbed. See Molecular properties.
Scatter plotA chart that places each molecule as a dot using two chosen properties for its position, so relationships and outliers between those two traits become visible. Can add a trendline with R². See Visualization gallery.
SMILESA compact text "recipe" that spells out a molecule's atoms and bonds as a line of characters — for example aspirin is CC(=O)OC1=CC=CC=C1C(=O)O. The most direct way to type a structure into the app. See Input formats.
StandardizeAn optional step that rescales every property onto a common scale before grouping, so a big-number property (like molecular weight in the hundreds) does not drown out a small-number one (like LogP). Recommended when grouping on several properties at once. See Grouping (GMM).
StereocenterAn atom where the same connections can be arranged in two mirror-image ways, giving rise to different 3-D forms of the molecule. The count flags how much stereochemistry a compound carries. See Molecular properties.
Synthetic Accessibility (SA)An estimate of how hard a molecule would be to make in the lab, on a scale of 1 (easy) to 10 (hard). Useful for prioritizing compounds that are realistic to synthesize. See Molecular properties.
Thiol-reactiveAn interference flag for compounds carrying reactive groups (like Michael acceptors, acylating agents, or alkyl halides) that can covalently grab cysteine residues on proteins, causing non-specific effects.
TPSA (Topological Polar Surface Area)The total area of a molecule's polar (water-attracting) surface, in square ångströms. It predicts how easily a molecule crosses membranes, the gut wall, or into the brain — lower values cross more easily. See Molecular properties.
TrendlineA straight line drawn through a scatter plot to summarize the overall direction of the relationship, often labelled with an equation and R² to say how well it fits. See Visualization gallery.
Veber rulesA two-part drug-likeness guide for oral bioavailability: polar surface area (TPSA) ≤ 140 Å² and rotatable bonds ≤ 10. Compounds within both limits tend to be absorbed better.
Violin plotLike a box plot, but the sides bulge to show where values cluster — a fuller picture of a distribution's shape, including whether it has more than one hump. See Visualization gallery.
z-scoreA value rewritten as "how many standard steps it sits above or below the average." Standardizing a column turns its numbers into z-scores so every property is on the same footing. See Grouping (GMM).
