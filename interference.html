THE NUMBERS

Interference flags

Some molecules light up in assays for the wrong reasons. The app screens for five well-known troublemakers — and tells you which one a compound resembles, so you know where to look harder.

The problem: a "hit" that isn't real

An assay is a lab test that measures whether a compound does something useful — for example, blocking an enzyme or binding a protein target. When a compound scores well, we call it a hit. But not every hit is real. Some molecules produce a strong-looking signal through chemistry that has nothing to do with the biology you care about. They fool the assay.

These false positives waste time and money. A chemist can spend weeks chasing a compound that was never genuinely active. Interference screening catches the usual suspects before they cost you that time. It does this by recognizing substructures — small chemical fragments inside a molecule — that are known to misbehave.

A flag is a heads-up, not a verdict

An interference flag does not mean a compound is bad or that your result is fake. It means look more carefully at your assay data for this one. Many flagged molecules turn out to be perfectly genuine hits. The flag simply tells you which kind of trickery to rule out — with the right follow-up experiment — before you trust the number.

How the screening works

The app reads each molecule's structure and compares it against libraries of known "troublemaker" patterns. A pattern here is a chemical shape written in a search language (chemists call these SMARTS patterns — a way to describe "any molecule containing this fragment"). If your molecule contains a matching fragment, it gets flagged in that category.

Each flag reports two things:

  • The matched pattern — which specific fragment was found, so you can see exactly why the compound was flagged.
  • A severity level — how strong the warning is (high, medium, or low), based on how reliably that fragment causes trouble.
Five independent screens

The five categories below are checked separately. A single molecule can be flagged by none, one, or several at once — a quinone, for instance, may trip both the redox-active and PAINS screens. Each tells you about a different failure mode.

1. PAINS — the frequent hitters

PAINS stands for Pan-Assay INterference compoundS ("pan" = across everything). These are roughly 480 substructures that researchers Baell and Holloway identified in 2010 after noticing that certain chemical shapes kept showing up as hits in assay after assay — against targets that have nothing in common. The app checks for them using RDKit's built-in FilterCatalog, the standard published library of these patterns.

What it catches: molecules containing one of those notorious fragments — common examples are certain rhodanines, hydroxyphenyl hydrazones, and catechol-bearing scaffolds.

Why it fools you: a "frequent hitter" appears active because of its general reactivity or its tendency to interfere with the detection method — not because it genuinely engages your target. If the same shape "hits" dozens of unrelated targets, the simplest explanation is that it's gaming the readout, not that it's a miraculously broad drug.

Example: a rhodanine-containing compound (a five-membered ring with sulfur and an exocyclic double bond) will trip the PAINS filter. Rhodanines are famous for looking active everywhere, which is exactly why they're on the list.

Severity: usually High — these are well-documented, repeat offenders.

2. Aggregator — the colloidal trap

Some greasy (water-hating) compounds don't dissolve neatly. At assay concentrations they clump together into tiny particles called colloids — think of oil droplets too small to see. These particles have a large, sticky surface that sequesters (soaks up and traps) the very protein your assay depends on. With the protein partly tied up, the enzyme looks inhibited — but the compound never touched its active site.

The app uses the Shoichet-lab heuristics: rules of thumb based on greasiness (cLogP — calculated grease score), molecular weight, and aromatic ring count, since aggregation is most common among large, greasy, aromatic molecules.

Why it fools you: the inhibition is real-looking in the tube, but it's caused by physical clumping, not molecular binding. The tell-tale sign: add a small amount of detergent (like Triton X-100) and the colloids fall apart — and the "inhibition" vanishes.

Example: a high-cLogP, multi-ring compound that inhibits three different enzymes equally well at micromolar concentrations is a classic aggregator suspect.

Severity: usually Medium — easy to confirm or rule out with one detergent control.

3. Thiol-reactive — the covalent grabbers

An electrophile is a "electron-hungry" fragment that readily reacts with electron-rich partners. Many proteins have a cysteine residue with an exposed sulfur atom (a thiol), and that sulfur is a prime target. A thiol-reactive compound forms a covalent (permanent) bond to it, sticking to the protein for good.

The app screens for about 15 electrophile patterns, following Dahlin et al. (2015): Michael acceptors (a double bond next to a carbonyl that thiols add across), acylating agents (groups that dump an acyl onto the sulfur), and alkyl halides (carbon–halogen bonds the sulfur displaces).

Why it fools you: the compound looks like a potent inhibitor, but it's just non-specifically gluing itself to any accessible cysteine — including ones outside the active site, and on proteins you never meant to test. That's promiscuity, not selectivity. (Note: deliberate targeted covalent drugs exist and are valuable — the flag flags indiscriminate reactivity, which you must verify.)

Example: an unsubstituted maleimide or a reactive α,β-unsaturated ketone will trip this screen as a Michael acceptor.

Severity: usually High — uncontrolled covalent reactivity is a serious liability.

4. Redox-active — the electron cyclers

Redox is short for reduction–oxidation: the gain and loss of electrons. A redox-active fragment can cycle — pick up electrons, hand them off, and repeat — generating reactive oxygen species (aggressive molecules like hydrogen peroxide) in the assay well. Those byproducts can damage the target protein or the detection reagents, producing an apparent effect that has nothing to do with binding.

The app checks about 10 patterns, following Proj et al. (2022): quinones (a ring with two carbonyls that flips between oxidized and reduced forms), catechols (a benzene ring with two neighboring OH groups), and nitro groups (an N bonded to two oxygens).

Why it fools you: the readout changes because the compound is chewing up the assay chemistry, not because it's a real inhibitor. Many redox-active hits also produce hydrogen peroxide that quietly inactivates the enzyme.

Example: 1,4-benzoquinone (O=C1C=CC(=O)C=C1) is the textbook redox cycler — and it commonly trips PAINS too.

Severity: usually Medium — confirmable by adding a reducing agent or a catalase control.

5. Fluorescent — the glowing decoys

Many assays read their result with light: a target activity is converted into a glow (fluorescence — a molecule absorbing light at one color and re-emitting it at another), and a detector measures how bright it is. The problem: some compounds are themselves dyes. If a test compound glows at the same color the assay watches, its own light gets added to (or subtracted from) the real signal, throwing off the number.

The app screens about 13 patterns, following Su et al. (2015): coumarins, fluoresceins / xanthenes (the classic bright dye scaffolds), anthracenes, and stilbenes — all well-known fluorophores (light-emitting fragments).

Why it fools you: the assay can't tell your compound's glow apart from the reporter's glow. A bright compound can look like an inhibitor (if it quenches the reporter) or an activator (if it glows along with it), purely as an optical artifact.

Example: a fluorescein-like xanthene scaffold or a coumarin core will trip this screen — these are literally the dyes used as labels in many assays.

Severity: usually Low–Medium — often dodged simply by switching to a non-optical readout.

What to do when something is flagged

A flag is the start of an investigation, not the end of one. The right follow-up confirms (or clears) the suspicion with a single orthogonal experiment. Here's the practical playbook by category.

Run an orthogonal assay

Re-test the compound with a different detection method — one that doesn't share the same weakness. A genuine hit shows up in both; an artifact usually shows up in only one. (Especially important for fluorescent and redox flags.)

Measure a dose–response curve

Test a range of concentrations. Real binding gives a clean, S-shaped curve. Aggregators and reactive compounds often give steep, "all-or-nothing" or erratic curves — a tell-tale sign something physical, not specific, is happening.

Add a detergent control

For aggregator flags, repeat the assay with a small amount of detergent. If the "inhibition" disappears, the compound was clumping — not binding. This single control resolves most aggregator suspicions.

Confirm the mechanism

For thiol-reactive flags, check whether activity drops when you add free thiol (e.g. glutathione) to the buffer — a covalent grabber gets mopped up. For redox flags, add a reducing agent or catalase and see if the effect fades.

Don't throw a flagged compound away on sight

Some of the most interesting hits carry a flag. The flag just tells you which control to run first. A flagged compound that survives the right orthogonal test is more trustworthy than an unflagged one you never stress-tested.

Where to find this in the app

Interference screening runs automatically in Single Molecule mode (alongside the property breakdown) and can be included as columns in Batch Processing for a whole library at once. Results are color-coded by severity — red high, amber medium, low — and each category expands to show exactly which pattern matched.

Reading the output

"No flags" means none of the five known patterns matched — reassuring, but not a guarantee of cleanliness (the screens cover famous troublemakers, not every possible one). A flag, conversely, means "investigate," not "discard."

Frequently asked

Does a flag mean my compound is inactive?

No. It means the compound contains a fragment known to sometimes cause false signals. Plenty of flagged compounds are genuinely active. The flag tells you which control to run to confirm the activity is real.

Can one molecule trigger several flags?

Yes. The five screens are independent. A quinone, for example, may trip both redox-active and PAINS. Each tells you about a different way it could be fooling you.

Are the patterns the app uses the standard published ones?

Yes. PAINS comes from RDKit's FilterCatalog (Baell & Holloway, 2010); the other four draw on the Shoichet aggregator heuristics and the Dahlin (2015), Proj (2022), and Su (2015) substructure sets. Every flag cites the matched pattern so you can trace it.

What if nothing is flagged?

Good news — your molecule doesn't contain any of the famous troublemaker fragments. But these screens cover the known offenders, not every conceivable artifact, so a clean result is encouraging rather than a certificate of innocence.
